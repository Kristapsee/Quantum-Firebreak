Nearly 85 percent of wildland fires in the United States are caused by people, not nature.
Common human wildfire causes include unattended campfires, debris burning, equipment sparks, discarded cigarettes, and arson.
Lightning is a major natural ignition source, and long-duration hot lightning is especially likely to start fires.
In many wildfires, embers rather than flame fronts are the main reason homes ignite.
Home ignition zone planning focuses on reducing nearby fuels so embers have fewer places to start spot fires.
Clearing dry leaves, needles, and debris from roofs, decks, and gutters lowers ember ignition risk.
Defensible space around buildings gives firefighters a safer working area and can slow fire spread.
A practiced evacuation plan with multiple routes improves survival when fire behavior changes quickly.
When evacuation is ordered, leaving early is one of the strongest life safety decisions.
In fast-moving fires, responders may prioritize getting people out alive over defending every structure.
NIFC records show tens of thousands of human-caused wildfires occur in the U.S. each year.
NIFC data also shows lightning-caused fires are fewer in number but can burn very large areas.
The 2018 Camp Fire in California became one of the deadliest and most destructive U.S. wildfires.
Investigators reported the Camp Fire ignition was linked to electrical transmission equipment failure.
During the Camp Fire response, thousands of firefighters and extensive air and ground resources were deployed.
Australia's 2009 Black Saturday bushfires killed 173 people and remain among the nation's worst fire disasters.
Black Saturday investigations linked major ignitions to power infrastructure failures, arson, and lightning.
The 2013 Yarnell Hill Fire in Arizona was ignited by dry lightning during severe heat, drought, and wind.
Nineteen Granite Mountain Hotshots died on Yarnell Hill, and they are widely honored for service and sacrifice.
The lone Granite Mountain survivor was positioned as a lookout and survived because he was outside the entrapment zone.
The 1949 Mann Gulch Fire killed 13 firefighters and became a landmark case in wildland fire training.
At Mann Gulch, foreman Wag Dodge survived by lighting an escape fire, a tactic now taught in wildfire safety history.
Lessons from Mann Gulch helped shape modern firefighter safety doctrine and watch-out training culture.
Heroic wildfire response often includes both front-line suppression and lifesaving civilian evacuations under extreme conditions.
